About Conway’s Game of Life

Conway’s Game of Life is a zero-player game invented by John Conway in 1970. It consists of an infinite grid of cells which, based on simple rules, can live, die, or multiply. Each cell’s fate is determined by its eight neighbors:

  • Underpopulation: A live cell with fewer than two live neighbors dies.
  • Survival: A live cell with two or three live neighbors lives on.
  • Overpopulation: A live cell with more than three live neighbors dies.
  • Reproduction: A dead cell with exactly three live neighbors becomes alive.

Despite its simple rules, the Game of Life produces complex patterns, oscillators, and even self-replicating structures.

Complexity vs Simplicity: The Game of Life raises questions about the nature of complexity, order, and chaos.It demonstrates that order can emerge from disorder and that seemingly simple rules can lead to unpredictable outcomes. It has applications in computer science, mathematics, and even philosophy.
